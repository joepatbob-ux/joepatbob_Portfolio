I came up through brand design — identity, packaging, print — work where the canvas is fixed and every decision has to earn its spot before it ships. That instinct carried into product design with new constraints stacked on: hardware tolerances, firmware limits, regulatory requirements, OEM relationships, platform debt. I still take brand projects, because brand (how someone experiences a product before they use it) and UX (how they experience it once they do) come down to the same thing — the feeling a product leaves behind — and splitting them across separate owners is usually where a product starts to feel disjointed.

That mix keeps paying off in unexpected places: the Verdant segment character set was as much a typography problem as a UI one, the packaging system I've pushed across Sensi and Verdant is a brand-coherence argument that doubles as a manufacturing-efficiency one, and the patents grew out of asking what a product should say when the hardware can't behave normally — a brand question aimed at a firmware problem.

**A brand question inside a firmware problem**

Sensi Lite's low-power shutdown sequence is the clearest version of this: when the display goes dark, the product still has to say something coherent through the phone instead of going silent. That's a firmware and interaction design problem on its face, but underneath it's the same brand question — what does this product sound like when it can't behave normally — that shows up everywhere else in the portfolio.
