I came up through brand design: identity, packaging, print. It is work where the canvas is fixed, every decision has to earn its spot, and once the thing ships there is no patching it.

That instinct carried straight into product design. It just had new constraints to work against — hardware tolerances, firmware limits, regulatory requirements, OEM relationships, installation workflows, platform debt. I still take brand projects, because the line between the two disciplines has always struck me as artificial. Brand is how someone experiences a product before they use it; the UX is how they experience it once they do. Both come down to the same thing, the feeling a product leaves behind, and the moment you hand them to separate owners with separate goals is usually the moment the product starts to feel disjointed.

That mix keeps paying off in places I did not see coming. The segment character set I drew for Verdant was as much a typography problem as a UI one. The packaging system I have been pushing across Sensi and Verdant is a brand-coherence argument that doubles as a manufacturing-efficiency one. The patents grew out of asking what a product should say when the hardware cannot behave normally, which is really a brand question aimed at a firmware problem. From the outside, none of this looks much like design work. It all comes from the same place, though.
