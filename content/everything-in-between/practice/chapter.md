Good design sits where form and function meet, and that doesn't stop at the screen. The contractor mounting the thermostat, the developer building the feature, the sales rep demoing it, the support agent taking the call, each one is having an experience of something that was designed. Leave those out and the design isn't finished. A product users love but sales can't explain is leaving money on the table. A feature that tests well but developers dread maintaining isn't really done. A flow that works in the app but falls apart during installation isn't a good experience, whatever the screens look like.

**Battery drill, not screwdriver**

I think of AI as a production tool, not an idea tool. It's a battery drill: great for getting a lot done fast, driving prototypes, documentation, and the material stakeholders need to see, so alignment can happen before a decision gets locked instead of after. But a drill strips the screw if you use it for fine work. What's worth building, which constraint outranks the others, when a familiar workflow is quietly carrying regulatory risk, that's still screwdriver work: slow, deliberate, by hand. That part doesn't compress. That part is the job.

**Thinking past the current system**

Say a hospital or grocer gets a shipment that spoils faster than expected, but nothing triggered an alert: shipping temperature stayed in range the whole trip. The warehouse it sat in before shipping had a smaller issue, not enough to alarm alone, that combined with transit conditions to shorten the product's life. No single system catches that, since each only sees its own slice. Connect the full lifecycle and the pattern becomes visible, so the customer can adjust handling instead of reacting after the loss. It's a concept I've developed on my own, not pitched yet, the same "don't wall off problems that are secretly the same problem" logic as Kelvin, one level up: interface to data.

**The parts that don't show up on a roadmap**

I make stickers for products and features I've worked on, watch coworkers put them on laptops, and more than once had someone see one and ask, "wait, you worked on that? Tell me about it." I've hosted a crawfish boil for the dev team and put together sales kits that have nothing to do with a screen. None of it is a deliverable, and none of it reaches the end user directly. But a developer who's proud of what they built maintains it with more care. A sales rep who's excited about a feature sells it more accurately instead of overpromising. A support agent who understands it explains it better. That excitement travels, and eventually the user feels it, even if they never see a single sticker.
