Good design sits where form and function meet, and that doesn't stop at the screen. The contractor mounting the thermostat, the developer building the feature, the sales rep demoing it, the support agent taking the call — each is having an experience of something that was designed, and leaving those experiences out means the design isn't finished. A product users love but sales can't explain is leaving money on the table; a feature that tests well but developers dread maintaining isn't really done; a flow that works in the app but falls apart during installation isn't a good experience, whatever the screens look like.

AI has collapsed the distance between making a decision and having something to show for it — I lean on it for working prototypes, documentation, and the material stakeholders need to see, so alignment can happen before a decision is locked instead of after. What it doesn't do is make the calls: what's worth building, which constraint outranks the others, when a familiar workflow is quietly carrying regulatory risk, when a surface is one feature away from being too crowded to hold together. That part doesn't compress. That part is the job.
