Four products had grown up separately, each built by its own internal team to solve its own users' problems, none of them talking to the others. Every one worked well enough to keep running, and that was exactly the trap.

When software is already making money, the argument for polishing its UX loses, over and over, to the argument for shipping something new. The cost of leaving it alone never shows up on anyone's dashboard, while the cost of changing it is right there in front of you — doubly so when users have regulatory SOPs that point at specific screens. In TempTrak, redrawing a screen is not only a design call; it can trigger documented retraining and an SOP revision. So features kept getting bolted on wherever it was easiest, year after year. TempTrak ended up with several ways to do the same task, each a leftover from a different team's fix, all of them kept around because pulling any one might break somebody's routine. Complexity piled up the way it does in old infrastructure: every decision made sense on its own, and the sum of them made the thing miserable to get around.

Kelvin was the response to all of that build-up. Not a rebuild from scratch, but a shared language that lets the four products drift toward consistency without any of them having to stop working while it happens.
